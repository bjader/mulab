Theory

Background and theory

X-rays interact with atoms to produce characteristic and continuous radiation. In this experiment for the first section you will focus on characteristic radiation. The count rate of measurements will affect the amount of noise that your results have which will distort the results significantly for one of the analysers. Doing a short scan will enable you to see the extent of this. Throughout the experiment ensure that you choose appropriate count times for each section of the experiment, some will be shorter than in the lab script and others will be longer. When calibrating the spectrometer use the auto calibration on the display of the equipment.

Before starting the experiment you should be familiar with:

  • X-ray absorption specta
  • X-ray spectrometers
  • Characteristic radiation including K and L series
  • Monochromatic radiation
  • Linear absorption coefficients
  • The Beer-Lambert Law

In the experiment electrons are fired at the source which produces characteristic and continuous radiation. These x-rays are produced in random directions, the crystal analysers diffract the x-rays through different angles according to Bragg’s law which are measured using a Geiger Muller detector.

Apparatus and Equations

Important Equations:

Moseley's law:

$$ E_n = Rhc (Z-\sigma)^2 \frac{1}{n^2}.$$

Where $R$ is Rydberg's constant, $\sigma$ is the screening constant, $h$ is Planck's Constant, $c$ is the speed of light, $Z$ is the atomic number, and $E_n$ is the binding energy of an electron in the $nth$ shell.

Bragg's Law:

$$ n\lambda = 2d\sin(\theta_{n}).$$

where $d $ is the crystal spacing, $n$ is the order of diffraction (e.g. 1,2,3,4...), $\lambda$ is the wavelength and $\theta_{n}$ is the angle of reinforced reflection.

Planck-Einstein Relation:

$$ E = \frac{hc}{\lambda}.$$

where $h$ is Planck's constant, $c$ is the speed of light, and $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the x-rays.

The angular dispersion (from differentiating Bragg's Law):

$$ \frac{\Delta\theta}{\Delta\lambda} = \frac{n}{2dcos\theta}.$$

The Beer-Lambert Law:

$$ I = I_0 e^{-{\mu t}}.$$

where $I$ = detected intensity, $I_0$ = emitted intensity (from x-ray source), $\mu$ is the linear absorption coefficient, and $t$ is the thickness of the absorbing material.

The dead time correction:

$$ N = \frac{N_0}{(1-\tau.N_0)}.$$

where $N_0$ is the measured count rate, $N$ is the true count rate and $\tau$ is the dead time (for the PHYWE 4.0 spectrometer, given to be $\sim{90}\mu s$).

The formula for propogating the uncertainty on Z is:

$$\Delta Z=\sqrt{(\frac{\partial Z}{\partial A})^2 (\Delta A)^2 + (\frac{\partial Z}{\partial B})^2 (\Delta B)^2 +...}.$$
