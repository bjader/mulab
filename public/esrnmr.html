Theory

This phenomenon of electron spin resonance used in this experiment involves the transition of an optically active unpaired electron. When atom is placed in an external magnetic field, this causes the energy levels to split. This is called the Zeeman Effect.

ESR involves a resonant coupling of the transition between Zeeman levels and an external oscillating electromagnetic field. When atom is placed in an external magnetic field, this causes the energy levels to split. This is called the Zeeman Effect. Electrons have charge and an intrinsic angular momentum called spin and thus must have a magnetic dipole moment, µ, antiparallel to the spin vector

$$\mu ={g_s}{\mu_B} \qquad (1) $$

Where ${g_s}$ is the Lande g factor and ${µ_B}$ is the Bohr magneton

When a sample is exposed to electromagnetic (EM) radiation while in a magnetic field, the electron in the lower Zeeman energy level absorbs a quanta of EM radiation and transitions to the higher energy Zeeman level. The difference in energies of the two levels and the condition for resonance corresponds to:

$$hv ={g_s}{\mu_B}B \qquad (2)$$

Where $h$ is Planck’s constant and $v$ is the EM field frequency.

This energy difference is found by bombarding photons with energy that is equal to the difference in energy of these two Zeeman levels.



This energy shown in equation (2) is quantized because the magnetic moment and the total angular momentum can only take discrete orientations relative to the magnetic field. Each orientation of the angular momentum corresponds to a state with a particular potential energy in the magnetic field. The difference in these energies is found directly using electron spin resonance. So if the energy of this alternating field is equal to the difference in energy between two bordering energy levels and equation (2) is satisfied, then the alternating field causes the magnetic moments from one orientation of the magnetic field to flip to another.

Theory

Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) is based on the magnetic properties of the nucleus. Like in ESR where the electron has an associated spin, the protons and neutrons also have an associated spin which means a nucleus that contains an unpaired proton and/or neutron has a magnetic dipole moment, $\mu$.

$$ \mu = g {\mu_n}$$

Where ${\mu_n}$ is the nuclear magneton and $g$ is the g factor.

This means that the theory outlined for ESR also applies to nuclei as well as electrons. When nuclei with spins of ½ such as protons are placed in a magnetic field, the spin axes of the nuclei undergo precession about the field direction instead of obediently flipping over and the magnetic moments aligning in the field direction. The frequency of this precession is known as the Larmor frequency, w, which is identical to the transition frequency.

$$\omega = \gamma B$$

Where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ration and $B$ is the applied magnetic field

This means that if a nucleus of spin ½ is in a magnetic field, then the nucleus is in its lower energy level. When a magnetic field is present, the axis of rotation will precess around the magnetic field and its frequency is given in equation (4). The potential energy of this precessing nucleus is given by,

$$E = \mu B cos(\theta)$$

Where $E$ is the energy and $\theta$ is the angle between the applied magnetic field and the axis of rotation.

So if the nucleus absorbs energy, then this angle will change. This means that for a nucleus, absorption of this radiation flips the magnetic moment so that it opposes the applied field and transitions to its higher energy state at a resonance frequency. This effect is known as Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR).

Apparatus

Electron Spin Resonance

Apparatus

Figure 1 consists of a Leybold didactic GMBH ESR basic unit (SE-9635) and it is driven by the Leybold didactic GMBH ESR control unit (SE-9636).

The ESR control unit provides all the required voltages needed to drive the probe unit and the Helmholtz coils, provides a digital readout of the radio frequency oscillation created by the probe units and it provides outputs for a dual trace oscilloscope.

The output as shown on the Gould two channel 20MHz oscilloscope OS300 is proportional to the current in the radio frequency oscillator, and is used to observe the resonance pulses. The other output is proportional to the current that is supplied to the Helmholtz coils, and indicates the magnitude of the magnetic field.

The ESR basic unit (SE-9635) consists of a pair of Helmholtz coils with bases that are connected parallel to each other and is used to provide a highly uniform magnetic field in which to place the sample material for the ESR measurement.

The equipment set up and main components for this experiment are shown in Fig.1. The experiment also consists of an ammeter which is used to record the current flowing 2I through the Helmholtz coils at the resonant frequencies and a Gauss meter attached to a Hall probe placed between the Helmholtz coils to ensure that the value of the magnetic field varies with coil current.

The DPPH is readily supplied in a small test tube which fits inside one of the small, medium and large copper inductance coils. Once an oscillating voltage is applied this generated a radio frequency radiation. Three copper coils of a different number of turns N are provided in this experiment and spans a range of frequencies driven by the ESR control unit. The small coil has 3 turns, the medium coil has 11 turns and the large copper inductance coil has 22 turns. These give values for the Figure 1: The main components of the ESR experiment which show the ESR control unit and a basic unit with a coil holding a sample attached, an oscilloscope to display the ESR signal and two Helmholtz coils connected in parallel. resonant frequency displayed on the ESR control unit and the current flowing through the Helmholtz coils 2I on the ammeter. One of these coils is plugged into the socket on the Basic Unit to form part of an oscillator circuit which can be driven by the Control Unit at frequencies between 15 and 130MHz.

When the frequency matches the electron spin resonance frequency of the specimen the radiation from the coil will be strongly absorbed. This changes the impedance of the oscillating circuit, reducing the voltage at the coil and thus creating a signal which can be detected using an oscilloscope. A picoscope (PC oscilloscope) is provided for capturing the oscilloscope traces for printing out and analysis.

The external magnetic field, B, is generated by a pair of Helmholtz coils powered through the ESR Control Unit. This field has two components: a static field B0, which can be varied from 0 to about 4 mT by varying the coil current, and an alternating component at the mains frequency of 50Hz. The function of the alternating B field is to sweep the maximum field back and forwards through a small range of values, which makes it easier to detect the exact field value at which resonance occurs. (The frequency of this modulation is less than one millionth of the frequency of the radiation so the resonance condition will not be perturbed).

In general, there will be a time delay between the signal from the copper coil reaching the oscilloscope and the modulated magnetic field. This means that the two signals arrive at the oscilloscope out of phase with one another. The control unit includes an adjustable phase shift (labeled φ) to compensate for this effect.

Method

Setup of apparatus:

  • Set up the Helmholtz coils parallel to each other and the correct distance apart.
  • Connect the Helmholtz coils in parallel to each other and in series with the ammeter to the ESR control unit. Make sure the current is running in the appropriate directions through both coils. Use the gaussmeter to check that the value of the magnetic field between the coils varies appropriately as a function of coil current.
  • Connect the ESR basic unit to the ESR control unit with the 6-pin DIN cable.
  • Using BNC cables, connect the output Y of the ESR control unit to channel 1 of the two-channel oscilloscope and the output X to channel 2.
  • Insert one of the copper coils into the ESR basic unit and place the DPPH specimen inside the coil.
  • Position the ESR basic unit so that the specimen is in the correct position between the Helmholtz coils.
  • Connect the picoscope to the computer using the USB cable. Use BNC T connectors and BNC cables to input the "ESR SIGNAL"͟ to channel A and the "$\Delta$B SIGNAL" to channel B of the picoscope.

Determining the Lande g factor for DPPH:

A suggested procedure for initial measurements is as follows:

  • Mount the copper coil with the smallest number of turns in the Basic Unit
  • Turn on the ESR control unit. The unit senses automatically which coil has been fitted to the base unit and it will set the frequency automatically to a value close to the expected resonance
  • Set the modulation amplitude $U_mod$ to the second scale marking
  • Set the phase shift $\phi$ to zero
  • Set the oscilloscope to dual channel operation and choose an appropriate time base
  • Slowly increase the field of the Helmholtz coils by adjusting the voltage U0 until the resonance signals reach their maximum intensity. Fine adjustments can be made by varying the frequency slightly, using the knob on the top of the base unit.
  • Switch the oscilloscope to x-y mode and adjust the phase shift so that the two resonance signals coincide i.e in phase.
  • Vary the Helmholtz coil voltage until the resonance signal is symmetric. Set the modulation voltage as low as possible.
  • Record the current 2I0 through the Helmholtz coils and the resonance frequency, with their uncertainties
  • Change the frequency and repeat the procedure
  • Plot a graph of resonance frequency against B0 and use this to determine a value of the Landé g factor for DPPH.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

Apparatus

The Leybold NMR supply unit (514 602) supplies the high frequency oscillating circuit in the measuring cell and the modulation coils of the NMR apparatus. It also works by amplifying the resonance signal during the observations of the transitions of the nuclear spin in an external magnetic field induced by high frequencies..

The Leybold AC/DC constant current power supply 0-15 V (512 501) was used a constant current source from up to 5A. The two outputs on the supply are isolated from each other, ungrounded and protected against short circuit and external voltages. The Leybold NMR probe (541 606) consists of a pair of modulation coils, set of NMR samples and a pair of holding clamps. The sample is placed inside the sample holder. Also used in the experiment is a Leybold oscilloscope 30MHz, two channel, analogous (575 214). It displays the NMR signal which can be saved as a trace on the picoscope.

In this experiment you will use an electromagnet to provide the large static Bz and a set of smaller coils to add a small sinusoidal modulation ΔBz. As in the ESR experiment, the modulation will allow you to pass periodically through the exact NMR resonance condition at a fixed excitation frequency and thereby visualize the resonance on the oscilloscope in x-y mode.

The electromagnet consists of two large copper coils (480 turns) which will be used to magnetize a U-shaped soft iron core. Two small modulation coils are mounted on the pole-pieces of the magnet, and the brass NMR probe unit is mounted between the poles. A rod-shaped solid specimen or a small glass test-tube containing a liquid can be fitted vertically into a small hole at the top of the probe and held in place by a rubber O-ring.

Method

Setup of apparatus:

  • Connect the two electromagnet coils to the DC power supply
  • Connect the modulation coils to the “MODULATION” output of the NMR supply unit
  • Using a BNC cable, connect the NMR probe to the input “PROBE” on the NMR supply unit
  • Using BNC cables, connect the output “NMR SIGNAL” to the Y channel of the two-channel oscilloscope and the output “ΔB SIGNAL” to the X channel of the oscilloscope.
  • Connect the picoscope to the computer using the USB cable. Use BNC T connectors and BNC cables to input the “NMR SIGNAL” to channel A and the “ΔB SIGNAL” to channel B of the picoscope.

NMR measurements on hydrogen in glycerine

Set the electromagnet coil current to zero.

Place the glycerin sample in the specimen chamber and adjust the O-ring so that the sample is positioned in the region of maximum field.

Set the controls on the NMR supply unit as follows:

  • Set the NMR Oscillator Amplitude knob on the left-hand side of the NMR supply unit front panel to its minimum value (fully anti-clockwise)
  • Set the NMR Oscillator Frequency knob to its maximum value (fully clockwise)
  • Set the B-modulation Amplitude knob on the right-hand side of the NMR supply unit front panel to its maximum (fully clockwise).
  • Turn on the NMR supply unit. No frequency should be displayed.
  • Slowly turn the NMR Oscillator Amplitude knob clockwise until the LED above it glows. A frequency of approximately 15MHz should now be displayed
